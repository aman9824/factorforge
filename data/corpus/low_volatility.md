# The Low-Volatility Anomaly

## Summary
The low-volatility factor buys low-risk stocks and sells high-risk ones. Counter to the textbook
risk-return tradeoff, low-volatility stocks have delivered higher risk-adjusted returns, an anomaly
usually explained by leverage constraints.

## Definition
The low-volatility factor ranks equities on trailing return volatility (or beta) and goes long the
low-volatility quantile while shorting the high-volatility quantile. This is a low-minus-high (by
risk) cross-sectional, dollar-neutral long/short factor.

## Empirical evidence
Ang, Hodrick, Xing, and Zhang (2006) documented that high-volatility stocks earned abnormally low
returns in U.S. equities. Baker, Bradley, and Wurgler (2011) argued that leverage-constrained
investors bid up volatile stocks, leaving low-volatility stocks underpriced. The low-volatility
factor has positive risk-adjusted returns across many equity markets.

## Regime behavior

### Recessions
The low-volatility factor is strongly defensive and outperforms in a recession because low-risk
stocks fall less in a market decline.

### Market recovery
The low-volatility factor tends to lag in a sharp market recovery because the high-risk stocks it is
short rebound the most.

## Risks and caveats
The low-volatility factor often carries large sector bets toward utilities and staples, so its
premium can be partly a sector or interest-rate exposure rather than a pure anomaly. It can also
become expensive and crowded after defensive stocks have rallied.
