# Quality and Profitability

## Summary
The quality factor buys highly profitable, stable firms and sells unprofitable ones. It has earned a
positive premium in U.S. equities and tends to be defensive, holding up better in downturns.

## Definition
The quality factor ranks equities on gross profitability (or a composite of profitability, growth,
and safety) and goes long the high-quality quantile while shorting the low-quality quantile. The
profitability variant is the RMW factor (robust minus weak), a dollar-neutral long/short factor.

## Empirical evidence
Novy-Marx (2013) showed that gross profitability has roughly the same predictive power for the
cross-section of equity returns as book-to-market. Fama and French (2015) added profitability and
investment factors to form their five-factor model. The quality premium has been positive in U.S.
equities and complements the value factor.

## Regime behavior

### Recessions
The quality factor is defensive and tends to outperform in a recession because profitable,
low-leverage firms weather downturns better than fragile, unprofitable firms.

### High-volatility regimes
Quality also tends to do well in high-volatility regimes as investors flee toward safer, profitable
companies (a flight to quality).

## Risks and caveats
Quality definitions are not standardized, so different profitability and safety composites give
different results, which raises the risk of selecting whichever definition looks best in sample.
A quality strategy can also become crowded and expensive after a long run of outperformance.
