# The Value Premium

## Summary
The value factor buys cheap stocks and sells expensive ones, where "cheap" is measured by a high
book-to-market ratio. Value has earned a long-run premium in U.S. equities, but the premium is
regime-dependent and has suffered prolonged drawdowns.

## Definition
The value factor ranks the cross-section of equities on their book-to-market ratio and goes long
the high book-to-market quantile while shorting the low book-to-market quantile. This high-minus-low
construction is the HML factor. Value is a cross-sectional, dollar-neutral long/short factor.

## Empirical evidence
Fama and French (1992) documented that the value factor explains a large share of the cross-section
of average equity returns. The value premium was strongly positive in U.S. equities from 1926 to
2006. The premium is widely interpreted as compensation for bearing distress risk.

## Regime behavior

### High-inflation regimes
The value premium has historically been strongest in high-inflation regimes. Value stocks behave
like short-duration cash flows, so they outperform growth stocks when inflation rises.

### Recessions
During a recession the value factor can underperform because cheap stocks are often financially
distressed firms most exposed to a downturn.

## Risks and caveats
The value factor experienced a severe and prolonged drawdown in U.S. equities from 2007 to 2020,
which some researchers attribute to a structural decline and others to a temporary regime. A high
book-to-market ratio can signal a value trap rather than a bargain. Claims of a stable value premium
should be treated with caution given the risk of data mining.
