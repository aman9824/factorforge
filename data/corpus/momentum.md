# The Momentum Factor

## Summary
The momentum factor buys recent winners and sells recent losers. It has produced one of the largest
and most persistent premia in U.S. equities, but it is prone to rare, severe crashes.

## Definition
The momentum factor ranks equities on their trailing twelve-month return, skipping the most recent
month, and goes long the high-momentum quantile while shorting the low-momentum quantile. This is a
cross-sectional, dollar-neutral long/short factor, sometimes labelled WML (winners minus losers).

## Empirical evidence
Jegadeesh and Titman (1993) documented that the momentum factor earned significant abnormal returns
in U.S. equities. Carhart (1997) added momentum as a fourth factor alongside the market, size, and
value factors. The momentum premium has been strongly positive across most equity markets.

## Regime behavior

### High-volatility regimes
Momentum is most dangerous in high-volatility regimes. Momentum crashes cluster in panicked,
volatile markets where the short leg of losers rebounds violently.

### Market recovery
During a sharp market recovery the momentum factor can experience a crash because the prior losers
it is short rally the hardest.

## Risks and caveats
The momentum factor suffered large crashes in 1932 and 2009, each during a market recovery from a
deep decline. Momentum has high turnover, so transaction costs materially erode the paper premium.
A momentum strategy fit to one sample can fail out of sample, so overfitting is a real concern.
