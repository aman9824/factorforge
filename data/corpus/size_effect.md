# The Size Effect

## Summary
The size factor buys small-cap stocks and sells large-cap stocks. A small-cap premium was documented
in early studies but has weakened substantially in later samples, making size a cautionary tale.

## Definition
The size factor ranks equities on market capitalization and goes long the small-cap quantile while
shorting the large-cap quantile. This low-minus-high (by market cap) construction is the SMB factor
(small minus big), a cross-sectional, dollar-neutral long/short factor.

## Empirical evidence
Banz (1981) documented that small-cap stocks earned higher average returns than large-cap stocks in
U.S. equities. Fama and French (1993) included the size factor as one of their three factors. The
small-cap premium was strongest before 1981 and much weaker afterward.

## Regime behavior

### Recessions
The size factor tends to underperform in a recession because small-cap stocks are more fragile and
sell off harder than large-cap stocks when liquidity dries up.

### Market recovery
Small-cap stocks often lead in the early stage of a market recovery, so the size factor can rebound
strongly coming out of a recession.

## Risks and caveats
The size premium largely disappeared after its discovery, a classic example of a factor that may
have been partly a product of data mining or that was arbitraged away. Small-cap returns are also
contaminated by illiquidity and survivorship effects. Treat any standalone size premium skeptically.
